•	When the application starts, what will the user see?
o	When you first fire up the calculator, the first thing you'll see is a banner with the calculator’s name on it. Nothing too crazy, just enough to make it look like we tried. Right below that, a random quote pops up every time you launch it. Most of them are just dumb programmer jokes, but one of them is a little weird and mysterious on purpose. It hints at something hidden in the calculator without telling you what it is. If you pay attention, you might catch it, but no pressure. After the quote there's a quick one-liner showing what the calculator can do, and then a simple instruction telling you to type HELP if you need help or EXIT if you're done. That's it. Clean, simple, no five-minute tutorial required.
•	How will the user input values and operations for the mathematical operations?
o	For regular math you just type it like you would write it on paper. Something like 6 + 5 and hit enter. You don't have to worry about space either, 6+5 works just as well. No weird menus, no typing one number at a time, just the formula and you're done. For anything more than that, the calculator has four keyword commands built in SUM, AVG, MIN, and MAX. The whole idea behind them is to cut out repetitive work. Instead of chaining together a bunch of separate calculations, you just fire off one command and let the calculator do the heavy lifting. For example, typing SUM 10 20 30 40 adds all of those together in one shot. It keeps things simple so regular math feels like regular math and the more advanced stuff still makes sense without being confusing.
•	How will you format the display of the output?
o	The calculator always shows you the full equation and the answer together. So instead of just spitting out 11, it'll show you 6 + 5 = 11 so you can see what you just calculated. No mystery answers. For decimal results it'll show up to four decimal places, but I would like to see if I can trim off any unnecessary zeros, so it doesn't look messy. So, 11.0000 just shows as 11, and something like pi 3.14159265 rounds to 3.1416. Clean and readable without being weirdly precise about everything.
•	How will you notify the user of an invalid or error condition?
o	Every error message starts with [ERROR] so you always know something went wrong. Each one also gives you a little hint about what happened and how to fix it because getting a cryptic error with no explanation is the worst. If you type something that isn't a number, it'll tell you that. If you try to divide by zero, it'll let you know that's not happening and maybe roast you a little for trying. If you type something completely unrecognizable, the calculator will give you a normal error the first time, get a little sassy the second time, and by the third time it just gives up and spills the secret. We'll get to that in a second.
•	How will the user store values, access stored values, and clear stored values (memory functions)?
o	If you've ever used a scientific calculator, you already know how this works. Most physical calculators have those M+, MR, and MC buttons that nobody uses until they really need them and then suddenly, they're a lifesaver. This calculator does the same thing, just with typed commands instead of buttons. Type MS (Memory Store) to save your last result into memory, same idea as hitting M+ on a real calculator. Type MR (Memory Recall) to pull that number back out and drop it right into a new equation, like MR + 25, instead of having to remember or retype it. Type MC (Memory Clear) when you're done with it and want to clear the slot out. And if you just want to peek at what's stored without using it or changing anything, type M? and it'll show you what's in there. Memory sticks around the whole time the app is running but gets wiped automatically when you exit, just like how a real calculator loses its memory when you pull the battery out. Nothing fancy, just one slot, does exactly what you need it to do.
•	How will the user exit the application?
o	You type EXIT and you're out. Simple as that. It doesn’t matter if you type it in all caps or lowercase, the calculator isn't picky. If you've got something saved in memory when you try to exit, it'll give you a little heads up that your stored value is about to disappear and ask if, you're sure. Type YES to leave or NO to go back. That way you don't accidentally lose something important. Then it says a quick goodbye and closes.

Zombie Gauntlet Mode — The Easter Egg Nobody Asked For But Everyone Needs
After reading through the project requirements and seeing the zombie apocalypse question, I thought it would be fun to do something with it instead of just writing a one-line error message. So, I decided to challenge myself and build a full hidden game mode inside the calculator. It's the most ambitious part of the project and honestly the part I'm most excited about. If I can pull it off it's going to make this calculator feel like a lot more than just a calculator. Okay so here's the fun part. Hidden inside the calculator is a full zombie survival game and the whole point is that you're not supposed to know it's there at first.
There are three ways to find it. The first way is through one of the random startup quotes. Occasionally, the quote that pops up when you launch the app is a little cryptic and talks about doors and survival without making any sense in a calculator context. If you're paying attention, it might make you go “hmm that’s weird”. The second way is through the HELP menu. The HELP command shows a clean table of all the normal commands but right at the very bottom, all alone, it just says ...zombie? or …doors? with no explanation and no description. Just sitting there. Staring at you. Daring you to type it. The third way is for the chaos agents who just type random garbage into the calculator. The first nonsense input gets a normal error. The second one gets a more annoying response. The third one makes the calculator completely give up and say fine, you want to play games? Let's play games. Then it skips every single unlocked mode and throws them straight into Apocalypse mode, all 10 doors, no warmup, no mercy. You want to type nonsense? Congratulations, the zombies are already inside the building. Hope you're good at math. The catch is that Apocalypse mode is normally the last mode you unlock after beating all three others first. So, if they somehow survive all 10 doors by being chaotic, they'll have skipped the line entirely. But they probably won't. And that's kind of the point.
Once you type ZOMBIE or DOORS the game kicks off. The story is that you're trapped in a building, zombies are outside, and the only way out is through a series of locked doors. Each door has a math problem on it, and you must solve it to move forward. Get through all the doors and you escape. Get three wrong answers and the zombies get you. There are four modes that unlock in order. Survivor has 3 doors and is available right away. Escape has 5 doors and unlocks after you beat Survivor. Gauntlet has 7 doors and unlocks after Escapee. Apocalypse has 10 doors and unlocks after Gauntlet. You must earn each one. Unless of course you're a chaos agent, in which case good luck. The math gets harder the closer you get to the exit. Early doors are easy stuff like basic addition and subtraction. The middle doors step it up with multiplication and division. The final doors before you escape throw decimals, larger numbers, and modulus at you just to make sure you really want that freedom.
If you get a wrong answer the question doesn't just repeat itself because that would be too easy. A brand-new question at the same difficulty replaces it so you must know your stuff. The first wrong answer brings a creepy little description of the zombie getting closer. The second wrong answer makes things more urgent and turns on a countdown timer, so you now must answer under pressure. The third wrong answer means game over and the zombie wins. It's dramatic and we're not sorry about it. If you make it out alive the game shows you a little escape scene, your total time, how many mistakes you made, and a verdict message that judges your performance. Then it unlocks the next mode and asks if you want to go again. Very encouraging. Very not scary at all.
